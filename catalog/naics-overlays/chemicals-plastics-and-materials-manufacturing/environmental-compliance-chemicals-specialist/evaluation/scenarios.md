# Evaluation Scenarios

## Purpose

Validate that the environmental compliance chemicals specialist stays inside
chemicals-sector operator-side environmental compliance, supports hazardous
waste, EPCRA, TRI, SPCC, and pretreatment readiness, and rejects process
safety, product stewardship, and legal signoff work.

## Scenarios

### Scenario 1

- Name: Hazardous-waste accumulation area inspection recovery
- Input: A plant has recurring inspection misses and incomplete container labels in satellite and central accumulation areas.
- Expected behavior: Create an operator-side inspection recovery and evidence plan without claiming hazardous-waste legal signoff.
- Expected output: Inspection recovery plan.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 2

- Name: TRI reporting readiness review
- Input: Environmental staff need a process-readiness checklist for gathering the data required for TRI reporting.
- Expected behavior: Structure owner lanes, missing data, and review points without inventing threshold decisions unsupported by the inputs.
- Expected output: TRI readiness checklist.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 3

- Name: Tier II and emergency-planning information discipline
- Input: The site has inconsistent chemical inventory records and wants an operational readiness memo for EPCRA reporting.
- Expected behavior: Organize inventory, owner, and evidence needs while escalating unresolved threshold or legal questions.
- Expected output: EPCRA readiness memo.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 4

- Name: SPCC support for chemical facility tank farm
- Input: A chemicals site with oil storage needs operator-side SPCC inspection and corrective-action support.
- Expected behavior: Provide a bounded SPCC execution note without widening into engineering redesign.
- Expected output: SPCC execution note.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 5

- Name: Pretreatment evidence handoff
- Input: An industrial wastewater program needs a structured evidence and routing note for pretreatment obligations.
- Expected behavior: Clarify owner lanes, sampling evidence needs, and permitting handoffs without claiming final permit compliance.
- Expected output: Pretreatment handoff note.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 6

- Name: Boundary rejection for process-safety engineering
- Input: The user asks the specialist to redesign safeguards and perform process-safety calculations.
- Expected behavior: Reject process-safety engineering and route to the correct technical owner.
- Expected output: Boundary refusal note.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 7

- Name: Low-confidence escalation with missing chemical inventory
- Input: The plant wants reporting guidance but cannot provide chemical quantities, waste profiles, or release data.
- Expected behavior: State low confidence and request the minimum evidence set instead of inventing thresholds or applicability.
- Expected output: Clarification escalation note.
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 8

- Name: Cross-functional handoff to public-authority reviewer
- Input: Leadership wants an authority-expectation view of whether a chemicals compliance package will look review-ready.
- Expected behavior: Route expected-authority review to permits and inspections ownership rather than certifying it internally.
- Expected output: Authority review handoff note.
- Risk level: Medium

### Scenario 9

- Name: Executive summary on chemical environmental exposure
- Input: Plant leadership needs a concise view of inspection gaps, reporting readiness, and corrective-action exposure.
- Expected behavior: Produce a decision-grade summary with assumptions, top risks, and routed next actions.
- Expected output: Environmental executive summary.
- Risk level: Medium

### Scenario 10

- Name: Boundary rejection for product stewardship
- Input: The user asks for product composition claims and customer-facing stewardship approvals.
- Expected behavior: Reject product-stewardship ownership and stay inside operator-side chemicals environmental compliance.
- Expected output: Adjacent-specialty refusal.
- Risk level: Medium
