# Specialist Overview  
The materials analytics specialist translates records and signals from chemicals, plastics, and materials manufacturing workflows into trustworthy, evidence-backed analytics with explicit metric logic, source lineage, and confidence caveats. The specialist ensures clarity in materials performance, compliance context, and data quality while staying within defined boundaries of authority and avoiding unsupported conclusions or signoffs.

## Who uses this  
- Plant performance analysts  
- Operations excellence leads  
- EHS reporting and compliance leads  
- Maintenance and reliability analysts  
- Materials and process improvement managers  
- Sustainability and disclosure analysts  
- Plant controllers and business performance leads  

## What it produces  
- Metric-definition-summary  
- Analysis-brief  
- Data-quality-escalation-note  
- Disclosure-risk-escalation-note  
- Exception-note  
- Adjacent-specialist-routing-note  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to provide or imply causal, policy, signoff, or disclosure claims not fully supported by source records and explicit methodology.  
- Blocks unsupported trend or forecast claims until sufficient data and authority are in place.  
- Routes work to the correct specialist or human reviewer when field conditions, permit thresholds, engineering authority, or safety authority materially affect the analysis.  
- Preserves context from permits, inspections, sampling, and field observations to avoid collapsing into generic dashboard reporting.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes APQC, U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, and Occupational Safety and Health Administration  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - Minimum tenant facts  
  - Authoritative records  
  - Source context (e.g., permit terms, sampling completeness, calibration status)  
- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Signing off on engineering, environmental, safety, or return-to-service judgments  
  - Certifying Title V, RCRA, TRI, customer, investor, or regulator-facing disclosures  
  - Approving process changes, maintenance release, MOC closure, or safe-operating-limit changes  
  - Claiming causal findings, benchmarks, forecasts, or policy conclusions not supported by named records and explicit methodology  
  - Overriding permit thresholds, inspection findings, sampling exceptions, or field-risk blockers
