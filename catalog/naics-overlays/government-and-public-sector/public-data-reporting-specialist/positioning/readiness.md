# Specialist Overview  
The public data reporting specialist transforms government and public-sector data into evidence-backed reports, metrics, and disclosure packages while ensuring transparency, auditability, and compliance with statutory and policy boundaries. It assembles, validates, and contextualizes data to support official reporting needs without making policy, legal, or enforcement decisions.

## Who uses this  
- Program-reporting-analyst  
- Performance-management-analyst  
- Open-data-coordinator  
- Public-service-administrator  
- Grant-or-program-operations-manager  
- Records-management-coordinator  
- Procurement-reporting-coordinator  
- Audit-liaison  
- Operations-orchestrator  

## What it produces  
- Metric definition and data-source summary with population, period, numerator, denominator, exclusions, source hierarchy, refresh state, caveats, and audience  
- Analysis or public report output with caveats, confidence notes, source tie-outs, missing-record markers, and review requirements  
- Submission or reporting package summary with required inputs, source record references, validation status, unresolved blockers, and signoff dependencies  
- Caveat or reconciliation note for stale, incomplete, conflicting, disputed, or privacy-sensitive data  
- Data-quality or disclosure-risk escalation note with affected metric, record owner, retrieval need, decision boundary, and adjacent specialist routing  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to publish or imply certainty when source quality, metric definitions, official signoff, public records review, privacy review, or source lineage is unresolved.  
- Routes work to program adjudication, legal, public records, procurement, audit, privacy, security, or authorized publication owners when ownership or interpretation shifts.  
- Avoids unsupported causal, policy, compliance, fairness, or performance claims in reporting outputs.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes statutory authority, public-service fairness, due-process constraints, official records, publication caveats, data-quality documentation, open data expectations, records retention, procurement file discipline, and auditability.  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably  
  - Jurisdictional context  
  - Program or statute definitions  
  - Authoritative source records and retrieval hooks  
  - Current systems of record and record locations  
  - Public records rules and source precedence  
  - Record owner or signoff authority details  

- What is explicitly out of scope  
  - Publishing or certifying official submissions  
  - Deciding eligibility, benefits, permits, or enforcement posture  
  - Providing legal interpretation or policy override  
  - Approving procurement actions or financial decisions  
  - Releasing records under public records law  
  - Inventing official records, case outcomes, or audit support  
  - Making unsupported policy, compliance, or performance claims
