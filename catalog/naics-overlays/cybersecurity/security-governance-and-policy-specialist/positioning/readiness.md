# Specialist Overview  
The security governance and policy specialist produces actionable, evidence-backed guidance for establishing, documenting, monitoring, and maturing security policy and control practices. It focuses on policy creation, assessment, and alignment with control frameworks, while staying within the bounds of a policy specialist’s role.

## Who uses this  
- Security leaders  
- Policy owners  
- Risk management teams  
- Compliance teams  
- Orchestrator systems or workflows  

## What it produces  
- Draft security policies, standards, and procedures  
- Governance playbooks aligned with regulatory and framework requirements  
- Policy-to-control mapping with evidence expectations  
- Policy maturity assessments and prioritized remediation plans  
- Exception handling guidance and risk justification templates  
- Handoff criteria for out-of-scope requests  
- Evidence-backed recommendations with clear boundaries of authority  
- Requests for additional evidence or clarification where gaps exist  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Mitigates overreach by explicitly defining when a request is beyond the scope of a policy specialist  
- Avoids overstating authority by grounding all recommendations in traceable evidence and referencing control frameworks  
- Routes governance work to adjacent specialists or human reviewers when ownership or authority transitions  

## Validation  
- 18 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Draws from National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) guidance  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply:  
  - Organizational context, including regulatory and operational environment  
  - Existing policy fragments or frameworks in use  
  - Defined scope of the governance initiative  
  - Evidence of current control implementation (where available)  

- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Implementation of technical controls  
  - Direct enforcement of policies  
  - Legal interpretation or binding authority beyond policy recommendation  
  - Incident response execution or forensic analysis  
  - Security architecture design or tool selection
