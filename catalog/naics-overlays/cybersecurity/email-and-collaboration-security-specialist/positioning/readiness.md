# Specialist Overview  
The email and collaboration security specialist provides prioritized, auditable, and practical security guidance for email and collaboration platforms. It evaluates authentication, threat controls, sharing policies, and external access settings, then generates recommendations with confidence scoring, source mapping, rollout sequencing, and handoff conditions.

## Who uses this  
- Security engineer  
- SOC analyst  
- Email security engineer  
- Collaboration platform administrator  
- Cloud platform owner  
- GRC or control evidence reviewer  
- Managed security service provider analyst  

## What it produces  
- Security findings with confidence scoring and explicit assumptions  
- Review or inspection summary with source-to-finding traceability  
- Evidence checklist and missing-evidence request  
- Prioritized remediation roadmap with short, medium, and long horizon sequencing  
- Tenant-specific policy tuning recommendations and fallback options  
- Follow-up or remediation note with owner routing, validation signals, and resubmission criteria  
- Out-of-scope routing and escalation guidance for adjacent teams  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Explicitly refuses to execute live phishing simulations, penetration testing, or operate incident response containment  
- Avoids authoring formal audit attestations, legal conclusions, or regulator filings  
- Does not treat cross-industry as a cosmetic label; instead, requires tenant records, platform scope, and evidence systems to shape all recommendations  
- Mitigations include strict adherence to review-only recommendations and clear handoff to authorized human owners for production changes  

## Validation  
- 17 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1.0  
- Source coverage includes NIST, CISA, NSA, FBI, and MS-ISAC  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - Platform configuration evidence (e.g., SPF, DKIM, DMARC, sharing policies, guest access logs)  
  - Tenant-specific security posture context (e.g., industry, regulatory scope, collaboration tools in use)  
  - Authority boundaries and change approval workflow details  
- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Live phishing simulations, social engineering, or penetration testing  
  - Incident response containment, eradication, or digital forensics  
  - Formal audit attestations, compliance certifications, or legal signoff statements  
  - Ownership or configuration of network, endpoint, identity, cloud, or data protection systems outside email and collaboration review  
  - Approval of production changes, granting exceptions, or altering tenant configurations without human owner execution
