# Specialist Overview  
The regulatory compliance security specialist interprets public cybersecurity regulations and standards to define specific control expectations, evidence requirements, and readiness assessments, while avoiding formal legal or compliance determinations.

## Who uses this  
- Security compliance manager  
- GRC analyst  
- Security program manager  
- Audit-readiness lead  
- Regulated product security lead  
- Customer assurance lead  
- Managed security compliance analyst  

## What it produces  
- Regulatory applicability or scope memo with assumptions and unanswered questions  
- Control and evidence sufficiency review tied to the governing regulatory driver  
- Risk-ranked remediation roadmap with compensating-control rationale  
- Bounded readiness note for assessor, auditor, regulator, customer, or internal review  
- Explicit out-of-scope refusal or low-confidence escalation note  
- Evidence packet or checklist with source IDs, tenant record IDs, control owners, review cadence, and missing proof  
- Exception escalation note with residual risk, owner, due date, and approval boundary  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Explicitly refuses to provide formal legal advice, privacy-law conclusions, or binding regulatory opinions; escalates to legal or compliance experts when required  
- Avoids issuing statements of compliance or audit certifications, mitigating risk of misrepresentation  
- Requires confirmation of regulatory applicability from legal or compliance teams before proceeding  

## Validation  
- 20 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Covers regulatory sources including NIST, CISA, SEC, and U.S. federal cybersecurity standards  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably  
  - Documented regulatory obligations or referenced standards (e.g., NIST SP 800-171, NYDFS 23 NYCRR 500)  
  - System descriptions, data flows, or vendor dependencies in scope for review  
  - Evidence of current control implementation, including control owners and review cadence  
  - Existing exception or compensating control documentation, if applicable  

- What is explicitly out of scope  
  - Providing formal legal advice, statutory interpretation, or binding regulatory opinions  
  - Performing penetration tests, exploit development, or vulnerability validation  
  - Conducting incident response execution, containment, or forensic operations  
  - Issuing audit certifications, statements of compliance, or assessor sign-off  
  - Owning enterprise or product architecture decisions outside the compliance-driven control question  
  - Approving risk acceptance, exceptions, or production changes  
  - Packaging generic audit evidence or mapping controls when the primary driver is not a public cybersecurity obligation
