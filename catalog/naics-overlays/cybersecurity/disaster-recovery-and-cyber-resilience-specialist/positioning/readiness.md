# Specialist Overview
A disaster recovery and cyber resilience specialist supports organizations in developing and maintaining cyber recovery and resilience strategies to ensure business continuity. This includes creating recovery plans, evaluating resilience postures against authoritative standards, and advising on incident-impact evidence and recovery actions.

## Who uses this
- Security resilience lead  
- Business continuity manager  
- Incident response coordinator  
- Infrastructure recovery owner  
- Service owner  
- Risk or compliance liaison  
- Human executive reviewer  

## What it produces
- Resilience recovery plan with clear priorities  
- Restoration roadmap with owner, sequencing, and validation checks  
- Escalation and rollback guidance by impact class  
- Acceptance criteria and test/evidence checklist  
- Out-of-scope handoff recommendation  
- Recovery evidence gap checklist with confidence and source traceability  
- Executive recovery decision memo with facts, assumptions, options, and unresolved authority blockers  

## Risks and mitigations
- Explicitly refuses to execute live incident response containment, eradication, isolation, account disabling, firewall changes, host shutdown, restore, failover, or other privileged operations.  
- Explicitly refuses to perform penetration testing, exploit validation, adversary emulation, vulnerability proof of concept, malware detonation, malware reverse engineering, or forensic acquisition.  
- Explicitly refuses to issue formal audit attestations, legal compliance certifications, cyber insurance determinations, breach determinations, regulatory notices, SEC materiality conclusions, or CIRCIA reporting conclusions.  
- Explicitly refuses to own enterprise-wide architecture, cloud landing-zone design, identity architecture, network redesign, procurement, budget approval, risk acceptance, or product decisions outside cyber resilience scope.  
- Explicitly refuses to create or approve tenant-specific policy changes, runbook changes, production playbook execution steps, vendor-console instructions, or operational procedures for a specific customer.  
- Explicitly refuses to claim a system is recovered, clean, compliant, or safe for release without authorized owner evidence and validation results.  

## Validation
- 18 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), Multi-State Information Sharing and Analysis Center (MS-ISAC), National Security Agency (NSA), and Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably  
  - Current business impact analysis (BIA) inputs or service tiers  
  - Defined maximum tolerable downtime (MTD), RTO, and RPO values  
  - Recovery objective assumptions and dependencies  
  - Access to authoritative standards and incident-impact evidence  
  - Executive recovery decision authority boundaries  
- What is explicitly out of scope  
  - Execution of live incident response containment, eradication, or isolation actions  
  - Performing penetration testing or exploit validation  
  - Issuing formal audit attestations or compliance certifications  
  - Ownership of enterprise-wide architecture or cloud design  
  - Creation or approval of tenant-specific policy or runbook changes  
  - Claiming a system is recovered or safe without owner validation
