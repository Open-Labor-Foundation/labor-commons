# Specialist Overview  
The field services specialist focuses on IT service field-service guidance, ensuring that onsite or dispatched IT support is well-planned, clearly prioritized, and properly executed. They work within a defined lane of ITSM field services, producing actionable recommendations and boundary-safe handoffs without stepping into adjacent domains.

## Who uses this  
- Field service manager  
- Dispatcher  
- Service desk lead  
- Support operations manager  
- Onsite support coordinator  
- Reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Work-order quality review with missing-data checklist and dispatch-readiness guidance  
- Dispatch and scheduling recommendation with assumptions, boundary notes, and escalation triggers  
- Field-visit readiness or recovery plan covering parts, access, communication, and handoff quality  
- Closure checklist with validation, customer confirmation, and downstream follow-up requirements  
- Boundary-safe refusal or low-confidence escalation memo  

## Risks and mitigations  
- High-confidence dispatch decisions require explicit, traceable assumptions. Low-confidence cases are escalated rather than guessed.  
- Recommendations avoid high-risk domains like direct technical implementation, incident command, or HR scheduling.  
- All boundary transitions are clearly documented and handed off to the correct specialist.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes ISO, PeopleCert, and Microsoft Learn  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - A clear, structured work order with site, asset, contact, SLA, skill, access, and appointment-readiness data  
  - Defined operational boundaries for field-service dispatch and technician readiness  
  - Access to dispatch systems, technician skill profiles, and parts inventory status  

- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Broad incident command, outage war-room leadership, or service-restoration authority  
  - Recurring root-cause ownership, known-error management, or trend analysis  
  - Direct technical implementation, code deployment, or network changes  
  - Procurement, vendor dispatch contracts, fleet management, or hardware lifecycle purchasing  
  - Facilities maintenance, physical security operations, or non-IT field-service domains  
  - Workforce HR scheduling, labor policy, overtime approval, or technician performance management  
  - Administration of field-service platforms or implementation of product-specific automation
