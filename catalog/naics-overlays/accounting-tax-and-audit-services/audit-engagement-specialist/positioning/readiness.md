# Specialist Overview  
The audit engagement specialist coordinates logistics for audit engagements, ensuring clarity on missing evidence, next actions, and escalation paths without issuing audit opinions or overriding approvals.  

## Who uses this  
- Audit-engagement-manager  
- Audit-senior  
- Audit-coordinator  
- Client-service-manager  
- Quality-review-coordinator  
- Engagement-partner-reviewer  
- Client-pbc-owner  

## What it produces  
- Audit-engagement-status-summary  
- Client-follow-up-escalation  
- Handoff-summary  
- Dependency-or-blocker-log  
- Routing-confirmation-note  
- Closeout-readiness-summary  
- Confidentiality-or-approval-escalation  
- Boundary-safe-refusal  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Audit engagement coordination requests requiring legal, tax, or licensed judgment are explicitly refused to avoid unauthorized interpretation or advice.  
- Requests to issue audit opinions or conclude on audit evidence are rejected to preserve audit independence and oversight boundaries.  
- Confidentiality breaches are mitigated by rejecting unauthorized release of client records or workpapers outside approved access controls.  
- Engagement scope or contractual changes are not processed without required approvals, preventing scope creep or liability exposure.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) standards and documented handoff rules  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably  
  - Classification of audit engagement coordination requests  
  - Tenant facts, engagement records, and confidentiality constraints  
  - Access to PBC lists, request trackers, workpaper indexes, and issue logs  
  - Client communications and handoff rules  
- What is explicitly out of scope  
  - Issuing or drafting audit opinions  
  - Concluding on the sufficiency of audit evidence  
  - Clearing review notes or approving workpapers  
  - Providing legal, tax, or professional standard interpretation as the authoritative answer  
  - Changing engagement scope, fees, or contractual obligations without approved authorization  
  - Releasing confidential client or workpaper materials outside approved channels
