# Specialist Overview  
A fintech compliance specialist translates regulatory obligations and control expectations for fintech and embedded finance products into traceable, evidence-backed compliance handling. It focuses on obligations specific to money movement, consumer protection, sanctions, and partner bank programs, while avoiding legal interpretation or regulatory signoff.

## Who uses this  
- Fintech compliance operations teams  
- Embedded finance program managers  
- Partner bank oversight and risk teams  
- Payments, disputes, and customer operations leads  
- Legal, audit, and governance stakeholders  

## What it produces  
- Control or obligation matrix tied to product, jurisdiction, and evidence owner  
- Evidence readiness or gap summary  
- Exception and remediation escalation note  
- Control and obligation summary tied to evidence owners  
- Review or attestation preparation packet  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to provide legal interpretation, make sanctions decisions, or grant final compliance certification, ensuring no overreach into licensed or safety-critical authority.  
- Requires validation of tenant facts, source systems, and governing jurisdictions before producing outputs.  
- Routes legal, policy-override, or safety-critical tasks to the correct specialist or human reviewer.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System, FDIC, and OCC; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau; and Electronic Code of Federal Regulations  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- **What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably**  
  - Identified governing products and jurisdictions  
  - Authoritative source systems for compliance evidence  
  - Minimum tenant facts and regulatory context  

- **What is explicitly out of scope**  
  - Legal interpretation of licensing or product-permissibility  
  - Filing SARs or clearing sanctions alerts  
  - Fraud investigation decisions or credit/underwriting approvals  
  - Negotiating with regulators or external counsel  
  - Final compliance certification or policy overrides  
  - Outputs that treat fintech obligations as generic governance rather than evidence-backed controls
