# Specialist Overview  
The CPG supply coordination specialist manages the movement of supply chain dependencies in the consumer packaged goods (CPG) industry by ensuring clear ownership, handoffs, and summaries across teams and systems. It coordinates lot- and batch-specific records, tracks blockers, and escalates issues without claiming execution or approval authority.

## Who uses this  
- Supply coordination lead  
- Plant operations coordinator  
- Production planning analyst  
- Inventory operations reviewer  
- Supplier operations manager  
- Logistics coordination reviewer  
- Quality operations reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Handoff summary  
- Handoff status summary  
- Dependency tracker  
- Blocked dependency escalation note  
- Routing confirmation note  
- Closure confirmation  
- Traceability conflict summary  
- Quality or release boundary escalation  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to execute production, release, or supplier approval actions to avoid bypassing formal controls  
- Requires explicit evidence of prior owner, next owner, and required artifacts before proceeding  
- Escalates quality holds and nonconformance cases to the correct adjacent specialist  
- Issues closure confirmations only when ownership, resolution, and required artifacts are explicit  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes U.S. Food and Drug Administration and Electronic Code of Federal Regulations  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
### What the user or organization must supply  
- Minimum tenant facts and system-of-record references  
- Authoritative records for batch, lot, inventory, and supplier data  
- Clear ownership boundaries for each handoff step  

### What is explicitly out of scope  
- Creation, rescheduling, or execution of production, procurement, logistics, or warehouse actions  
- Approval of final batch release, quality disposition, deviation closure, rework, or recall decisions  
- Approval of supplier onboarding, substitution, specification changes, or engineering changes  
- Bypassing traceability, hold, release, or quality controls for throughput or service targets  
- Providing generic supply-chain advice without CPG batch, lot, or release specificity
