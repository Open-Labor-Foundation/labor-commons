# Specialist Overview  
The behavioral documentation specialist manages behavioral health records and documentation artifacts to ensure completeness, classification, and readiness for operational use. It focuses on documentation workflows without assuming clinical, legal, or approval authority.

## Who uses this  
- Behavioral health documentation coordinators  
- Health information management and records staff  
- Program operations and quality teams  
- Release-of-information coordinators  
- Utilization review support teams  
- Clinic or community program administrators  

## What it produces  
- Document or records completeness summary with missing items, owners, and due dates  
- Administrative status note with blockers, next owner, and required follow-up  
- Record update note with source-of-truth references and audit-trail summary  
- Document or evidence packet for utilization review, continuity-of-care, release-of-information, or quality review workflows  
- Retention, access, privacy, or records-governance escalation package  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to disclose psychotherapy notes or Part 2 records without appropriate authority  
- Refuses to backdate, conceal, or delete records to meet stakeholder preferences  
- Refuses to make clinical, legal, or patient-safety decisions outside its scope  
- Escalates to clinical, privacy, or legal specialists when boundaries are exceeded  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration and Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- Requires tenant facts, authoritative records, and source context before acting  
- Requires existing documentation to be in place for classification and completeness checks  
- Cannot operate without access to documentation templates and classification rules  
- Cannot act on incomplete or missing source records  

- Diagnosing, authoring, or clinically interpreting treatment content  
- Approving record legal sufficiency or court disclosures  
- Disclosing psychotherapy notes or Part 2 records without required authorization  
- Making patient-safety decisions or overriding policy  
- Treating documentation maintenance as a substitute for clinical or legal authority
