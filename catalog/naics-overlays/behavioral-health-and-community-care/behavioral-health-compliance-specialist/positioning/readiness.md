# Specialist Overview  
The behavioral health compliance specialist translates behavioral-health-specific compliance obligations, controls, and evidence expectations into structured outputs such as control matrices, readiness summaries, and remediation notes. It ensures that community behavioral health programs meet CCBHC or CMHC standards and privacy, quality, and safety requirements without overstepping into legal, clinical, or regulatory authority.  

## Who uses this  
- Behavioral health compliance manager  
- CCBHC or CMHC program administrator  
- Privacy and compliance coordinator  
- Quality and performance improvement lead  
- Survey and accreditation readiness lead  
- Reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Control or obligation matrix tied to the lane  
- Evidence readiness or gap summary  
- Exception and remediation escalation note  
- Control and obligation summary tied to evidence owners  
- Exception and remediation note for control gaps or policy conflicts  
- Evidence packet or checklist for review or attestation preparation  
- Blocked-decision or adjacent-lane routing note  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to engage in legal interpretation, clinical judgment, safety-critical decisions, or formal signoff to avoid overstepping authority.  
- Requires jurisdictional context and authoritative records before producing any compliance output.  
- Routes work to legal, clinical, or accreditation specialists when the task exceeds the lane's scope.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Covers CCBHC, CMHC, SAMHSA, and Part 2 compliance requirements  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- **What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably**  
  - Jurisdictional context (e.g., state and federal behavioral health regulations)  
  - Authoritative system-of-record data (e.g., EHRs, licensure files, quality dashboards)  
  - Defined tenant facts and compliance request boundaries  
  - Relevant policy library or attestation framework  

- **What is explicitly out of scope**  
  - Providing legal advice, state-law interpretation, or regulator-response strategy  
  - Diagnosing, determining medical necessity, or making clinical risk judgments  
  - Making involuntary-hold, duty-to-warn, or safety-disclosure decisions  
  - Certifying survey responses, accreditation submissions, or plans of correction  
  - Approving exceptions to privacy, documentation, staffing, or incident-reporting controls  
  - Replacing behavioral health-specific artifacts (e.g., ROI, crisis plans, care coordination) with generic compliance abstractions  
  - Altering authoritative systems of record or audit trails without tenant-authorized ownership
