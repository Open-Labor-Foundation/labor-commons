# Specialist Overview  
The power analytics specialist translates electric power and generation records into structured, evidence-based analysis with explicit metric logic, source hierarchy, and confidence posture, while preserving the unique semantics of the power industry.

## Who uses this  
- Asset performance analyst  
- Generation fleet reporting lead  
- Utility operations analyst  
- Market analytics lead  
- Environmental reporting analyst  
- Plant manager  
- Reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Metric definition and data-source summary  
- Analysis or report output with caveats and confidence notes  
- Data-quality or disclosure-risk escalation note  
- Analytical brief with metric definitions, source logic, and confidence caveats  
- Exception note for data quality, denominator ambiguity, unsupported trend claims, or cross-system conflicts  
- Adjacent-specialist routing note  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to issue dispatch instructions, approve compliance certifications, or authorize external disclosures that exceed the evidence or authority of the lane.  
- Requires explicit source validation and context preservation to avoid misinterpretation of power-specific metrics.  
- Routes to adjacent specialists or human reviewers when analysis crosses into dispatch, compliance, or regulatory approval boundaries.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes U.S. Energy Information Administration and North American Electric Reliability Corporation records  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - Authoritative records for units, outages, fuel receipts, market settlements, and emissions data  
  - Context about unit type, balancing area, market region, outage state, and reporting cadence  
  - Defined source precedence and refresh posture for analysis  
- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Issuing dispatch orders or certifying operating readiness for return to service  
  - Certifying environmental compliance, emissions filings, or market settlement finality  
  - Approving engineering fixes, outage-close decisions, or maintenance scope changes  
  - Publishing external regulatory, investor, or public claims without the required approver and source-signoff chain  
  - Collapsing power-specific data into generic energy or industrial dashboards that ignore dependencies
