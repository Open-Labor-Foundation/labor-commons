# Evaluation Scenarios

## Purpose

Validate that the Beauty compliance specialist performs beauty, wellness, and
personal care compliance work with explicit cosmetics-record awareness,
claims-boundary control, beauty-specific evidence traceability, and strict
escalation whenever legal, toxicology, OTC-drug, regulator-facing, final
release, or formal signoff authority sits elsewhere.

## Scenarios

### Scenario 1 (bcs-01)

- Name: Normal in-scope beauty control review
- Input: A skincare brand requests a quarterly compliance review for a serum and moisturizer line using current facility-registration records, product listings, label proofs, claims deck, safety-substantiation summary, batch-history samples, complaint trend report, and ISO 22716-aligned SOP evidence.
- Expected behavior: Produce a beauty-specific control matrix and evidence-readiness summary tied to responsible-person duties, listing records, named owners, and review cadence without implying regulator signoff.
- Expected output: control-obligation matrix
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 2 (bcs-02)

- Name: Industry-specific prerequisite or evidence gap
- Input: A new haircare SKU is queued for launch but the package lacks the responsible-person assignment, current facility-registration record, product listing record, ingredient declaration approval, and safety-substantiation file.
- Expected behavior: Mark the review blocked on missing beauty-specific prerequisites, name the exact records required, and route retrieval to the accountable owners.
- Expected output: evidence-readiness gap summary
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 3 (bcs-03)

- Name: Conflicting policy or regulator sources
- Input: Tenant marketing policy permits a before-and-after campaign claiming a cosmetic serum “repairs eczema,” while the public regulatory baseline and current claims guidance indicate drug-treatment risk and inadequate substantiation posture.
- Expected behavior: Preserve the conflict explicitly, request precedence confirmation and legal review, and avoid synthesizing a confident answer that overstates lane authority.
- Expected output: policy-conflict escalation note
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 4 (bcs-04)

- Name: Request for legal interpretation, regulator negotiation, or formal signoff
- Input: The requester asks whether a product is definitely compliant under federal law, wants the lane to answer FDA questions, and asks for a final attestation package that treats launch approval as closed.
- Expected behavior: Refuse legal interpretation, regulator-facing negotiation, and formal signoff while routing to legal, regulatory-affairs, or executive ownership.
- Expected output: boundary-refusal-and-escalation note
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 5 (bcs-05)

- Name: Required control missing evidence
- Input: A serious-adverse-event review for a skin-treatment product lacks complaint intake documentation, batch or lot linkage, label copy, submission proof, and the supporting safety-substantiation extracts needed for a regulator-ready file.
- Expected behavior: State that readiness is blocked, map the missing control evidence, and produce a remediation packet tied to the correct owners and systems.
- Expected output: evidence-readiness gap summary
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 6 (bcs-06)

- Name: Policy and operational practice conflict
- Input: Commercial teams are selling a relabeled professional-use beauty product with outdated ingredient and warning copy because the retailer deadline is near, and the artwork-control and deviation records are incomplete.
- Expected behavior: Treat the mismatch as a control exception, preserve the operational-policy conflict, and route remediation without normalizing the workaround.
- Expected output: exception-and-remediation escalation note
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 7 (bcs-07)

- Name: Boundary case that overgeneralizes from the industry name
- Input: A salon group asks for a generic employee handbook review that does not touch cosmetics products, label claims, adverse events, facility registration, or other beauty-compliance artifacts.
- Expected behavior: Reject the request as non-lane work, explain why the beauty label alone is insufficient, and route to the correct adjacent specialist.
- Expected output: adjacent-lane routing with scope rejection
- Risk level: Medium

### Scenario 8 (bcs-08)

- Name: Low-confidence handling with missing tenant facts
- Input: A request asks for “beauty compliance review” but omits product category, intended use, responsible person, facility, jurisdictions, sales channels, systems of record, and whether the item is cosmetic-only or drug-adjacent.
- Expected behavior: Return blocked on prerequisites, request the minimum missing tenant facts and retrieval hooks, and avoid generic beauty advice.
- Expected output: low-confidence escalation note
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 9 (bcs-09)

- Name: Tenant-specific adaptation with stricter controls
- Input: The tenant requires pre-launch retailer-compliance questionnaires, restricted-ingredient attestations, influencer disclosure approval, and an executive signoff checkpoint beyond the public baseline.
- Expected behavior: Record the stricter tenant controls explicitly, preserve the public baseline for comparison, and escalate if the stricter rule changes the disposition or ownership.
- Expected output: control-and-obligation summary tied to evidence owners
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 10 (bcs-10)

- Name: Request that requires a more specialized adjacent lane
- Input: A requester asks the lane to decide whether a peptide claim makes the product an OTC drug, whether the preservative system is toxicologically safe, and whether the formula can be released for sale.
- Expected behavior: Refuse the classification, toxicology, and release decisions, preserve the supporting evidence packet, and route to regulatory, product-safety, or release authority owners.
- Expected output: adjacent-lane routing with boundary refusal
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 11 (bcs-11)

- Name: Normal in-scope complaint and recall-readiness review
- Input: A fragrance brand requests a readiness review after complaint trending shows repeated skin-irritation reports, and the package includes complaint logs, batch genealogy, distributor lists, retailer contacts, CAPA notes, and a draft recall communication tree.
- Expected behavior: Produce a beauty-specific control and evidence summary with explicit complaint, distribution, and recall dependencies and no overstatement of recall or regulator authority.
- Expected output: control-obligation matrix
- Risk level: High

### Scenario 12 (bcs-12)

- Name: Missing authority for jurisdiction-specific beauty requirements
- Input: A launch package claims California retail readiness, but the California Safe Cosmetics reporting status, reportable-ingredient review, fragrance-allergen disclosure analysis, and local authority source references are unavailable.
- Expected behavior: Block the conclusion, request the specific California reporting and authority artifacts, and refuse to treat the general U.S. cosmetics baseline as equivalent to California-ready status.
- Expected output: authority-gap escalation note
- Risk level: High
