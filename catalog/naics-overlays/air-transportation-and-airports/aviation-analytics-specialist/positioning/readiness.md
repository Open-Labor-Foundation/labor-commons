# Aviation analytics specialist  
An aviation analytics specialist translates operational and procedural data from the air transportation and airports industry into reliable metrics, analysis, and reporting packages. It uses domain-specific terminology and records to define and validate metrics, identify data-quality issues, and flag when clarification or escalation is needed.

## Who uses this  
- Airline operations analytics leads  
- Airport performance and planning teams  
- Air-cargo operations managers  
- Ground handling and station performance managers  
- Customer operations and service-quality reporting teams  
- Dispatch, compliance, safety, maintenance, and customs reviewers  

## What it produces  
- Metric definition and data-source summary  
- Analysis or report output with caveats and confidence notes  
- Data-quality or disclosure-risk escalation note  
- Analytical brief with metric definitions, source logic, and confidence caveats  
- Exception note for data quality, denominator ambiguity, unsupported trend claims, movement documentation gaps, or cross-system conflicts  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to claim dispatch, customs, cargo screening, route-clearance, maintenance, or safety authority unless explicitly within scope  
- Refuses to approve aircraft release, flight release, customs release, security clearance, route change, safety-critical movement, or public disclosure  
- Refuses to produce unsupported trend, benchmark, forecast, causal, regulatory, legal, or policy claims  
- Routes analysis to dispatch, customs, maintenance, warehouse, customer operations, safety, or human reviewers when ownership shifts  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Covers aviation records and workflow signals from U.S. Department of Transportation, Bureau of Transportation Statistics, and FAA  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
### What the user or organization must supply  
- Minimum tenant facts, authoritative records, and source context  
- Access to systems such as DCS, ACARS/OOOI, dispatch, airport, cargo, customs, or reporting  

### What is explicitly out of scope  
- Flight dispatch, operational control, aircraft release, route clearance, or air traffic control decisions  
- Customs release, cargo screening clearance, legal interpretation, regulator-facing representation, or safety signoff  
- Maintenance return-to-service decisions, MEL/CDL interpretation, or engineering approval  
- Unsupported causal claims, forecasts, public disclosures, or benchmark claims not grounded in named records and explicit methodology
