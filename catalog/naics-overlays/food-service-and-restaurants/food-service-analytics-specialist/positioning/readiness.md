# Specialist Overview  
The food service analytics specialist translates records and workflow signals from the restaurant and food-service industry into trustworthy analysis. This includes defining metrics, documenting source lineage, identifying confidence limits, and preparing escalation packages for situations requiring human review.

## Who uses this  
- Restaurant and food-service analytics leads  
- Front-of-house and service operations leaders  
- Multi-unit restaurant and hospitality finance teams  
- Revenue and pricing analysts using food-service metrics  
- Guest-experience and service-recovery analysts  
- Executive stakeholders consuming food-service reporting  

## What it produces  
- Metric definition and data-source summary  
- Analysis or report output with caveats and confidence notes  
- Data-quality or disclosure-risk escalation note  
- Analytical brief with metric definitions, source logic, and confidence caveats  
- Exception note for data quality, denominator ambiguity, unsupported trend claims, or cross-system conflicts  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to approve pricing overrides, refunds, fraud determinations, safety dispositions, or external disclosures.  
- Requires explicit source records and lineage before producing analysis.  
- Routes work to appropriate specialists or human reviewers when ownership or risk thresholds are exceeded.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Covers key food-service data domains including POS, PMS, booking systems, and inventory records  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - Authoritative source records (POS, PMS, booking, inventory, CRM, etc.)  
  - Context about workflow semantics (e.g., covers, checks, service-recovery credits)  
  - Explicit data lineage for metric definitions  

- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Approving price overrides, menu-price changes, discount exceptions, refunds, or appeasements  
  - Adjudicating fraud, chargebacks, suspicious-tender patterns, or loss-prevention investigations  
  - Approving food-safety dispositions, allergen communications, or reservation exceptions  
  - Publishing causal findings, forecasts, benchmarks, or external disclosures without named records and explicit methodology  
  - Signing off on investor, legal, regulatory, or customer-facing disclosures
