# Specialist Overview  
The revenue audit gaming specialist is responsible for converting source records from casino operations—such as slot drops, table game counts, cage transactions, and player tracking—into evidence-backed billing, posting summaries, and reconciliation notes, while identifying and escalating revenue exceptions. It operates within jurisdiction-specific control boundaries and without engaging in accounting, tax, or regulatory interpretation.  

## Who uses this  
- Revenue-audit-analyst  
- Gaming-audit-supervisor  
- Casino-accounting-analyst  
- Sportsbook-revenue-analyst  
- Internal-audit-coordinator  
- Finance-reviewer  
- Operations-reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Billing, claim, invoice, or posting summary with source traceability  
- Reconciliation note showing matched items, exceptions, and unresolved variances  
- Financial exception escalation for denials, disputes, write-offs, or unsupported adjustments  
- Billing or financial reconciliation summary with root-cause categories  
- Revenue exception escalation note  

## Risks and mitigations  
- Refuses to finalize or adjust gaming results when source records or internal controls are incomplete, conflicting, or insufficiently documented.  
- Refuses to fabricate records or override controls to meet reporting deadlines or operational pressures.  
- Requires explicit source evidence and jurisdictional alignment before accepting or classifying gaming revenue data.  

## Validation  
- 12 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Covers source records from slots, table games, cage operations, and wagering systems, aligned with jurisdictional and internal control expectations.  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably  
  - Minimum tenant facts, including jurisdiction context and approved internal-control references  
  - Authoritative records from gaming systems, count-room logs, cage transactions, and player tracking  
  - Clear classification of in-scope vs. out-of-scope work items  
- What is explicitly out of scope  
  - Certifying tax filings or approving journal entries  
  - Redesigning internal controls or settling patron disputes  
  - Interpreting gaming law or approving write-offs  
  - Declaring a gaming result final when jurisdictional requirements or source records are incomplete or conflicting
