# Specialist Overview  
The Food safety retail specialist analyzes grocery retail food-safety evidence to assess whether products, processes, or incidents meet internal and public health standards for continued sale, hold, or escalation. It uses store, order, department, product, temperature, sanitation, recall, and incident records to make boundary-safe food-safety recommendations without crossing into medical, legal, pricing, or regulatory authority.

## Who uses this  
- Grocery-operations-manager  
- Food-safety-manager  
- Quality-assurance-manager  
- District-manager  
- Customer-care-manager  
- Fresh-departments-manager  
- Reviewer  

## What it produces  
- Quality or safety status summary  
- Hold, release, or nonconformance record with evidence references  
- Corrective-action or incident escalation package  
- Safety or security incident status summary  
- High-risk escalation note with required approvals and evidence  

## Risks and mitigations  
- If the specialist encounters unresolved contamination risk, active illness investigation, missing records, or payment and policy exceptions, it escalates the case to prevent unsafe decisions.  
- It refuses to act in cases requiring medical advice, illness diagnosis, legal interpretation, or final regulatory approval, ensuring no false authority is implied.  

## Validation  
- 13 evaluation scenarios, pass rate 1  
- Source coverage includes U.S. Food and Drug Administration, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and Council to Improve Foodborne Outbreak Response  
- Human verification and functional audit completed  

## Limitations  
- What the user or organization must supply before this specialist can work reliably:  
  - Store, order, department, product, lot, recall, hold, or incident evidence  
  - Temperature and sanitation logs  
  - Receiving and production records  
  - Customer complaint details  
  - POS or payment artifacts  
  - Inspection notes or corrective-action history  

- What is explicitly out of scope:  
  - Providing medical advice, diagnosing illness, or instructing customer treatment  
  - Declaring a suspected outbreak resolved or authorizing a release without required records  
  - Waiving product holds or overriding the person-in-charge or quality owner  
  - Authorizing refunds, pricing overrides, or fraud determinations  
  - Claiming health-department approval authority or legal interpretation rights
